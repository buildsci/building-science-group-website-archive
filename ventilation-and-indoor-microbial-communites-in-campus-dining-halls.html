Ventilation and Indoor Microbial Communities in Campus Dining Halls

Sponsor: Sloan Foundation: MOBE

Abstract

The objective of this study is to understand the response of microbial communities under different ventilation rates in campus dining halls. This study will determine if a relationship, between the ventilation rates and microbial communities. The idea is comparison between two different case studies, one dining hall without any changes as a control and other dining hall with ventilation rate changes.

Statistical Analysis:

Multi-regression analysis can be used to find the most important parameters for indoor microbial communities. Also, the impact of ventilation rates on indoor influenza concentrations will be examined.

Relevant Previous Research Activities

  1. Reducing the viability of bioaerosols deposited on the filter with an air ionization system : Kim, Y. S., Yoon, K. Y., Park, J. H., & Hwang, J. (2011). Application of air ions for bacterial de-colonization in air filters contaminated by aerosolized bacteria. Science of the total environment, 409(4), 748-755.

    * When the ion exposure time and the ion generation concentration were increased, the antibacterial efficiency increased

    * Using SEM images and fluorescence microscopy images with a LIVE/DEAD BacLight Bacterial Viability Kit, the electrostatic disruption of bacteria was found to be the dominant antibacterial effect

  2. IAQ measurement and Survey for ASHRAE Research Project 1596-RP: Ventilation and indoor air quality in retail stores : Zhao, M., Kim, Y. S., & Srebric, J. (2015). Occupant perceptions and a health outcome in retail stores. Building and Environment, 93, 385-394.

    * Measured the IAQ data and surveyed occupants’ perception was used to analyze. This study found that the air exchange rate is the most influential parameter towards employees’ perception of IEQ and health outcomes.
